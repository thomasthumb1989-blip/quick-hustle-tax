## What Counts as a Side Hustle?

A side hustle is any work you do to earn money outside your main employment. HMRC doesn't use the term "side hustle" — they call it **trading income**. Here are common examples:

- **Selling online**: Etsy, Vinted, eBay, Depop, Amazon FBA
- **Freelancing**: copywriting, web design, consulting, tutoring
- **Gig work**: Deliveroo, Uber, TaskRabbit
- **Content creation**: YouTube ad revenue, Twitch donations, Substack subscriptions
- **Renting**: Airbnb income (short-term property lets)
- **Crafts and products**: handmade goods, print-on-demand, digital downloads

If you're doing any of the above **regularly and for profit**, HMRC considers it trading income. Occasional one-off sales of personal items (like clearing out your wardrobe on Vinted) generally don't count — but regular selling does.

## The £1,000 Trading Allowance Explained

This is the single most important rule for side hustlers, and it's widely misunderstood.

Since April 2017, every UK taxpayer gets a **£1,000 trading allowance**. This means:

- If your **total gross trading income** (that's revenue, not profit) is **£1,000 or less** in the tax year, you **don't need to tell HMRC at all**. No Self Assessment. No tax return. Nothing.
- If your gross trading income is **above £1,000**, you **must register for Self Assessment** — but you can still use the £1,000 as a flat deduction instead of claiming actual expenses.

### Worked Example: Under the Allowance

Sarah sells handmade candles on Etsy. In 2026/27, she makes £800 in total sales. She spent £300 on wax and materials.

**Result**: Her gross income (£800) is below £1,000. She doesn't need to declare anything to HMRC. The £300 in expenses is irrelevant — she's automatically covered by the trading allowance.

### Worked Example: Over the Allowance

James does freelance graphic design alongside his day job. He earns £4,000 in 2026/27 and has £600 in actual expenses (software subscriptions, a drawing tablet).

James has two options:
1. **Claim the £1,000 trading allowance**: Taxable profit = £4,000 − £1,000 = **£3,000**
2. **Claim actual expenses**: Taxable profit = £4,000 − £600 = **£3,400**

Option 1 gives a lower profit, so James should claim the trading allowance. He saves tax on an extra £400 of income.

**Key rule**: You can claim the trading allowance **OR** actual expenses, but **never both**.

## When Do I Need to Register for Self Assessment?

You need to register for Self Assessment if **any** of these apply:

1. Your side-hustle gross income exceeds **£1,000** in the tax year
2. You want to claim **actual expenses** that exceed the trading allowance
3. You want to **claim a loss** to offset against other income
4. HMRC has asked you to file a return

To register, go to [GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment) and sign up. You'll get a **Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR)** — keep this safe, you'll need it every year.

**Important deadline**: You must register by **5 October** following the end of the tax year. For 2026/27 income, that means registering by **5 October 2027**.

## How Side-Hustle Income Stacks With Your Salary

This is where most people get confused. Your side-hustle income doesn't get its own separate tax calculation — it **stacks on top** of your employment income.

Here's why that matters:

Your employer already handles your Personal Allowance (£12,570) and basic rate tax through PAYE. When you add side-hustle profit on top, that extra income is taxed at whatever rate your total income puts you in.

### The Stacking Effect

Imagine you earn £48,000 from your job. After your Personal Allowance, your taxable employment income is £35,430 — all within the basic rate band (which goes up to £37,700 above your allowance, i.e. up to £50,270 total).

Now add £5,000 of side-hustle profit (after the trading allowance). Your total income becomes £53,000. The first £2,270 of your side-hustle profit fills the remaining basic rate band at 20%. The remaining £2,730 gets taxed at 40% (higher rate).

This is why the calculator above shows your **marginal rate** on the side hustle, not a flat 20%.

## Trading Allowance vs Actual Expenses — Which to Claim?

The decision is straightforward:

- **If your expenses are less than £1,000** → claim the trading allowance (it's a bigger deduction)
- **If your expenses are more than £1,000** → claim actual expenses
- **If you're not sure** → use the calculator above in "Auto" mode, which picks the better option for you

Common claimable expenses include:
- Raw materials and stock
- Software subscriptions (Canva, Adobe, accounting software)
- Home office costs (proportion of energy bills, broadband)
- Phone bills (business proportion)
- Travel to clients or markets
- Professional development and courses
- Advertising and marketing costs
- Packaging and postage

Keep receipts for everything. HMRC can ask to see records for **up to 5 years** after the filing deadline.

## Three Worked Examples

### Example 1: Casual Etsy Seller

- **Job**: Barista, £22,000/year
- **Side hustle**: Sells stickers on Etsy, £2,500 gross, £400 expenses

Uses trading allowance (£1,000 > £400 expenses). Profit = £1,500. All taxed at basic rate (20%) = **£300 income tax**. No Class 4 NI (profit below £12,570 threshold). Total to pay via Self Assessment: **£300**.

### Example 2: Freelance Web Developer

- **Job**: Marketing manager, £42,000/year
- **Side hustle**: Freelance web projects, £12,000 gross, £3,000 expenses

Claims actual expenses (£3,000 > £1,000). Profit = £9,000. Employment + profit = £51,000 → crosses into higher rate. Some side-hustle income taxed at 40%. Class 4 NI also applies. Total Self Assessment bill: roughly **£2,700**.

### Example 3: High Earner Content Creator

- **Job**: Software engineer, £95,000/year
- **Side hustle**: YouTube channel, £25,000 gross, £5,000 expenses

Claims actual expenses. Profit = £20,000. Total income = £115,000 → Personal Allowance starts to taper. Most side-hustle income taxed at 40%. Total Self Assessment bill: roughly **£9,500** including Class 4 NI.

## Common Mistakes Side Hustlers Make

1. **Thinking the trading allowance is on top of your Personal Allowance** — it's not. It's a separate £1,000 deduction from trading income specifically.

2. **Not registering for Self Assessment when they should** — if your gross trading income exceeds £1,000, you must register, even if your profit (after expenses) is small.

3. **Mixing up gross income and profit** — the £1,000 trading allowance threshold is based on **gross income** (total revenue), not profit after expenses.

4. **Claiming both trading allowance AND expenses** — you get one or the other, never both.

5. **Forgetting Class 4 National Insurance** — income tax isn't the only bill. If your side-hustle profit exceeds £12,570, you'll also pay 6% Class 4 NI.

6. **Not keeping records** — HMRC expects you to keep invoices, bank statements, and receipts for at least 5 years. Digital records are fine.

7. **Ignoring payment on account** — if your Self Assessment bill exceeds £1,000 and more than 20% of your total tax is unpaid via PAYE, HMRC will ask for advance payments towards next year's bill.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to pay tax on my side hustle if I already pay tax on my salary?

Yes, if your side-hustle income exceeds the £1,000 trading allowance. Your salary uses up your Personal Allowance through PAYE, so side-hustle profit is typically taxed from the first pound at your marginal rate (20% or 40%).

### What if my side hustle makes a loss?

If your expenses exceed your income, you can claim a trading loss. This can be offset against other income in the same year or carried forward. You'll need to file a Self Assessment return to claim the loss. Note: you cannot claim both a loss and the trading allowance — you must use actual expenses.

### Is selling on Vinted or eBay taxable?

It depends. Selling your own personal belongings isn't normally taxable. But if you're buying items to resell at a profit, or making items to sell regularly, HMRC treats that as trading income. Since January 2024, platforms like Vinted, eBay, and Etsy report seller data to HMRC if you make over 30 sales or earn over €2,000.

### Do I need an accountant for my side hustle?

Not necessarily. If your side hustle is straightforward (one source of income, simple expenses), Self Assessment is manageable with accounting software like FreeAgent, QuickBooks, or even a spreadsheet. For more complex situations (Ltd company, multiple income sources, VAT), professional advice is worth the investment.

### What happens if I don't declare my side-hustle income?

HMRC is increasingly effective at detecting undeclared income, especially with digital platform reporting requirements. Penalties range from 0% (unprompted disclosure) to 100% of the tax owed. If HMRC discovers it first, penalties are higher. It's always better to declare voluntarily.

### Can I use the trading allowance if I'm a landlord?

The trading allowance is for trading income only — not rental income. There's a separate **£1,000 property allowance** for rental income, which works similarly.

### How do I actually pay my Self Assessment bill?

You can pay through your GOV.UK account via direct debit, bank transfer, debit card, or at a bank/building society. HMRC also offers the option to spread payments through a **Budget Payment Plan** if you prefer to pay monthly rather than in a lump sum.

### Does my side-hustle income affect my student loan repayments?

Yes — if you're on a student loan, your side-hustle profit is added to your total income for repayment calculations. Student loan repayments through Self Assessment are calculated on your total income including side-hustle profit. This calculator doesn't include student loans yet, but a dedicated calculator is coming soon.
